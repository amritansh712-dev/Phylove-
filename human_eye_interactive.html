25 cm — Least Distance of Distinct Vision
The closest distance at which objects can be seen clearly without strain. For a healthy adult eye: 25 cm. Ciliary muscles are fully contracted; lens is at maximum curvature.
Far Point

The farthest point visible with complete clarity. For a normal eye: infinity. Ciliary muscles are fully relaxed; lens is thinnest and has maximum focal length.

04 — Dispersion of Light
White Light & the Rainbow Spectrum

A glass prism splits white light into its seven constituent colors (VIBGYOR). Drag the angle slider to see how the prism angle affects dispersion.

Prism Angle (A) 45°
Incident Angle 30°

VIBGYOR SPECTRUM

Key Fact

Violet bends most — shortest wavelength (~380nm), highest refractive index.

Red bends least — longest wavelength (~700nm), lowest refractive index.

🔬
Newton's Recombination Experiment: Newton used two identical prisms — the first dispersed white light into VIBGYOR. The second prism, placed inverted, recombined all colors back into white light. This conclusively proved white light is a mixture of seven colors.
Angle of Deviation
D = (i₁ + i₂) – A
D = deviation, i₁ & i₂ = angles of incidence/emergence, A = prism angle
Snell's Law at Interface
n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
Governs bending at each glass-air boundary. Different λ → different n → different bend.
Refractive Index
n = c / v
c = speed in vacuum (3×10⁸ m/s), v = speed in medium. Higher n → more bending.
05 — Atmospheric Phenomena
Why the Sky is Blue & the Sun is Red

Light interacts with atmospheric particles differently depending on wavelength. Select a phenomenon to visualize it.

Twinkling of Stars
Atmospheric Refraction
Stars are point sources. Continuously shifting atmospheric density layers cause their apparent position and brightness to fluctuate rapidly. Planets don't twinkle — they're extended sources, cancelling fluctuations.
Early Sunrise / Late Sunset
+2 Minutes of Extra Light
Atmospheric refraction bends sunlight below the horizon. We see the Sun ~2 minutes before it actually rises and ~2 minutes after it actually sets.
Tyndall Effect
Path of Light Made Visible
When light passes through a colloid (fine suspended particles), the beam path becomes visible due to scattering. Examples: car headlights in fog, beam through dusty room, blue appearance of smoke.
Scattering (Rayleigh)
Blue Sky, Red Sun
Particles smaller than wavelength scatter shorter wavelengths (blue/violet) far more. Blue sky: scattered blue reaches eyes from all directions. Red sunset: blue scattered away in long path, only red remains.
06 — Formula Reference
Complete Formula Ledger
Lens Formula
1/f = 1/v – 1/u
f = focal length, v = image distance, u = object distance. All measured from optical center.
Magnification (Lens)
m = h'/h = v/u
+ve m → virtual, erect image. –ve m → real, inverted image.
Power of Lens
P = 1/f(m) = 100/f(cm)
SI unit: Dioptre (D). Convex: +D. Concave: –D. 1D = 1 m⁻¹
Combined Lens Power
P = P₁ + P₂ + P₃…
For lenses in contact, net power is algebraic sum of individual powers.
Snell's Law
sin i / sin r = n₂₁
Ratio of sine of angle of incidence to sine of angle of refraction = constant for given pair of media.
Refractive Index
nₘ = c / v
c = 3×10⁸ m/s (speed in vacuum). Absolute refractive index of medium m.
Mirror Formula
1/f = 1/v + 1/u
For spherical mirrors. Sign convention: distances measured from pole P.
Magnification (Mirror)
m = –v/u
–ve → real inverted image. +ve → virtual erect image.
