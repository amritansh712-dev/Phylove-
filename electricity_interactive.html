01 · FOUNDATIONS

Electric Current & Potential

Core Concepts

Electric Current (I)

Electric current is defined as the rate of flow of electric charge across a cross-section of a conductor. If a net charge Q flows across any cross-section in time t, the current is:

I = Q / t

Since charge Q can be written as an integer multiple of elementary electronic charge: Q = n × e, where e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

I = ne / t
SI Unit
Ampere (A)

1A = 1 C/s (one coulomb per second)

Measured By
Ammeter

Connected in series — very low resistance

Electric Potential Difference (V)

The work done to move a unit charge from one point to another in an electric circuit.

V = W / Q
SI Unit
Volt (V)

1V = 1 J/C (joule per coulomb)

Measured By
Voltmeter

Connected in parallel — very high resistance

📌 IMPORTANT DISTINCTIONS

Conventional vs. Electron Current

Conventional current flows from + to −. Electrons actually flow from − to +. NCERT follows conventional direction.

Why Ammeter in Series?

Very low resistance ensures it does not alter the current in the circuit it measures.

Why Voltmeter in Parallel?

Very high resistance ensures negligible current flows through it, so it doesn't disturb the potential difference.

02 · THE MASTER LAW

Ohm's Law

Interactive

In 1827, Georg Simon Ohm established that the electric current (I) flowing through a metallic conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) across its terminals, provided its temperature remains constant.

V = I × R

Where R is the resistance — the property of a conductor to resist the flow of charges through it.

RESISTANCE FACTORS
Directly proportional to length (l)

Longer wire → more resistance. Electrons collide more.

Inversely proportional to area (A)

Thicker wire → less resistance. More paths for electrons.

Depends on material & temperature

Different materials have different resistivities (ρ).

R = ρ × (l / A)
RESISTIVITY VALUES (Ω·m)
Silver (Ag) 1.60 × 10⁻⁸
Copper (Cu) 1.69 × 10⁻⁸
Nichrome 1.00 × 10⁻⁶
Glass (insulator) 10¹⁰ – 10¹⁴
Rubber (insulator) 10¹³ – 10¹⁶

Alloys like nichrome do not oxidize at high temperatures → ideal for heating appliances (toasters, heaters, electric irons).

03 · NETWORKS

Resistance Combinations

Interactive

Series Configuration

Resistors joined end-to-end sequentially. The same current passes through all components; potential difference is shared.

⚡
Current (I) is constant

I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃

⚡
Voltage (V) is divided

V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃

Rₛ = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
Practical Application

Old Christmas fairy lights were in series — if one bulb fused, the whole string went dark! Modern lights use parallel connections.

Parallel Configuration

Resistors connected across the same two nodes. Potential difference remains same across all; total current is divided.

⚡
Voltage (V) is constant

V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃

⚡
Current (I) is divided

I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃

1/Rₚ = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ ...
Key Property

Equivalent parallel resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance. Adding more resistors in parallel always decreases total resistance.

Practical Application

All household appliances are connected in parallel! Each gets the full 220V, can be switched on/off independently, and adding more appliances doesn't dim existing ones.

04 · THERMAL DYNAMICS

Heating Effect of Current

Interactive

When current flows through a purely resistive conductor, the source energy is entirely converted to heat. This is the heating effect (Joule heating).

Joule's Law of Heating

Heat generated (H) in a resistor is:

  • ▸ Directly proportional to I² (square of current)
  • ▸ Directly proportional to R (resistance)
  • ▸ Directly proportional to t (time)
H = I² × R × t

Electric Power (P)

Rate at which electrical energy is consumed or dissipated:

P = V × I
P = I² × R
P = V² / R
UNITS & CONVERSIONS
Watt (W)

SI unit of power

kWh

Commercial unit of energy ("unit")

1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
APPLICATIONS OF HEATING EFFECT
💡
Electric Bulb

Tungsten filament (m.p. 3380°C) glows white-hot. Nitrogen/Argon filled to prevent oxidation.

🔥
Heating Appliances

Electric iron, toaster, geyser — use Nichrome wire (high resistivity, won't oxidize).

🔌
Electric Fuse

Thin wire of low m.p. alloy (Pb+Sn). Melts and breaks circuit if current exceeds safe limit.

07 · EXAM PREP

Important Points & Tips

⚡ MOST ASKED IN EXAMS
01.

If R₁ and R₂ are in parallel and one branch is disconnected → equivalent resistance increases.

02.

If wire is stretched to n times its length, resistance becomes n² times (area decreases, length increases).

03.

Alloys (nichrome, constantan) have high resistivity and don't oxidize at high temps → used in heating elements.

04.

Resistivity is independent of shape/size — it's an intrinsic material property. Resistance depends on shape.

05.

SI unit of resistivity = Ω·m (ohm-meter). SI unit of conductance = Siemens (S).

06.

Tungsten is used for bulb filaments because of its very high melting point (3380°C).

🧮 PROBLEM-SOLVING STEPS
Step 1

Identify the circuit type — is it series, parallel, or mixed? Draw a simplified diagram.

Step 2

Find equivalent resistance using the correct formula. Simplify from innermost combination outward.

Step 3

Apply Ohm's Law (V=IR) to find total current from the battery.

Step 4

Work branch-by-branch — use V=IR for each resistor knowing whether it's current or voltage that's shared.

Step 5

For power/heat: choose the most convenient form of P based on what's known (P=VI, P=I²R, or P=V²/R).
