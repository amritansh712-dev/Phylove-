What is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a self-sustaining functional unit of the biosphere comprising all interacting living organisms alongside the non-living physical environment.

Lindeman's 10% Rule

Only ~10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. The remaining 90% is lost as heat, respiration, and metabolic processes.

Key Fact: Green plants capture only ~1% of total solar energy falling on their leaves.

Biological Magnification

The progressive increase in concentration of toxic, non-biodegradable chemicals (like DDT) at each successive trophic level. Humans, as top consumers, are most severely affected.

Why does it happen? These chemicals cannot be metabolized or excreted — they accumulate in fatty tissues and intensify up the chain.

⚠️ Critical levels reached! At these concentrations, DDT causes reproductive failure, immune suppression, and neurological damage in top predators and humans.

What is DDT?

Dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane — a synthetic insecticide widely used in agriculture. It is highly stable, fat-soluble, and non-biodegradable, making it the classic example of biological magnification.

The Magnification Process

1

Chemical enters environment via agricultural runoff

2

Absorbed by producers and stored in fatty tissues

3

Each predator eats many prey — concentrations multiply

4

Top consumers accumulate dangerous levels — thousands of times the environmental concentration

🌊 Real Example: In aquatic ecosystems, DDT concentration can increase from 0.000003 ppm in water to over 25 ppm in fish-eating birds — a magnification of over 8 million times!

Ozone Layer Depletion

The stratospheric ozone layer absorbs lethal UV-C and UV-B radiation. CFCs, released by refrigerants and aerosols, catalytically destroy ozone molecules, creating dangerous holes.

🌍 Ozone Layer Thickness Simulator

Pre-1980 (Safe) Critical

⚗️ Ozone Formation Reaction

O₂ → UV → O + O

O₂ + O → O₃ (Ozone)

High-energy UV radiation splits O₂ into free oxygen atoms, which combine with O₂ molecules to form protective O₃ ozone in the stratosphere (~15–35 km altitude).

⚡ How CFCs Destroy Ozone

CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons) released from refrigerators, air conditioners, and aerosol sprays rise into the stratosphere. UV radiation breaks them down, releasing chlorine atoms that catalytically destroy ozone — one Cl atom can destroy 100,000 ozone molecules!

Sources: Refrigerants, fire extinguishers, aerosol propellants, foam blowing agents

☠️ Effects of UV Exposure

🩺Skin cancers and melanoma
👁️Cataracts and eye damage
🌾Crop damage and reduced yield
🐟Damage to marine phytoplankton
🌐
Montreal Protocol (1987)
UNEP — United Nations Environment Programme

The landmark international agreement successfully froze global CFC production at 1986 levels, leading to gradual ozone layer recovery — considered the most successful environmental treaty in history.

Key Concepts

🌿 Producers, Consumers & Decomposers — Roles Explained
expand_more

Producers (Autotrophs)

Green plants and photosynthetic bacteria. Trap solar energy to synthesize organic food from inorganic elements via photosynthesis. Form the base of every food chain.

Consumers (Heterotrophs)

Depend on producers. Herbivores (primary) → Carnivores (secondary/tertiary) → Omnivores. Cannot make their own food.

Decomposers

Bacteria and fungi. Break down dead organisms into simple inorganic nutrients, returning them to soil. Essential for nutrient cycling.

⚡ The 10% Law — Lindeman's Principle (1942)
expand_more

Proposed by Raymond Lindeman in 1942. States that only about 10% of the energy stored at one trophic level is transferred to the next higher trophic level. The remaining ~90% is used for:

🫀
Metabolic activities (respiration)
🌡️
Heat loss to environment
💩
Excretion & decomposition

Ecological implication: This massive energy loss at each step explains why food chains can support only 3–4 trophic levels in nature, and also why vegetarian diets are more energy-efficient.

🧪 Biological Magnification — DDT & Persistent Pollutants
expand_more

Definition: The progressive increase in the concentration of toxic, non-biodegradable chemicals at each successive trophic level of a food chain.

Why it happens: Unlike energy (which dissipates as heat), toxic chemicals like DDT are fat-soluble and cannot be metabolized or excreted. Instead, they accumulate in fatty tissues. As a predator eats many prey individuals, it concentrates their combined chemical load.

Example: DDT concentration in water: 0.003 ppb → Phytoplankton: 0.04 ppm → Small fish: 0.5 ppm → Large fish: 2 ppm → Fish-eating birds: 25+ ppm. The concentration increases by over 10 million times from water to top predator.

🌍 Ozone Depletion — Chemistry & Global Response
expand_more

The ozone layer (O₃) exists in the stratosphere at ~15–35 km altitude. Its structural thickness began dropping sharply in the 1980s due to synthetic CFCs.

Formation

O₂ + UV → O + O
O₂ + O → O₃

Destruction by CFCs

Cl (from CFCs) + O₃ → ClO + O₂
Each Cl atom destroys ~100,000 O₃ molecules

Montreal Protocol (1987): UNEP-brokered international treaty that froze CFC production at 1986 levels. Widely considered the most successful environmental treaty ever, with visible ozone layer recovery observed since the 1990s.

♻️ Solid Waste — Classification & Environmental Impact
expand_more

Modern industrial lifestyles generate enormous quantities of two fundamental waste categories with vastly different environmental destinies:

Biodegradable

Food waste, paper, natural fibres. Broken down by bacteria/fungi. Returns nutrients to ecosystem via decomposition cycle.

Non-Biodegradable

Plastics, glass, e-waste. Cannot be broken down. Persists for centuries, clogs drains, causes soil toxicity, contributes to biological magnification.

⚠️ Globally, the world generates ~2.5 billion tons of solid waste annually. Only ~13% is recycled. Mismanaged non-biodegradable waste is a leading cause of ocean pollution, with 8 million metric tons of plastic entering oceans every year.
