SECTION 01

Magnetic Fields & Field Lines

What is a Magnetic Field?

A magnetic field is a region surrounding a magnet or current-carrying conductor where its magnetic force can be detected. It is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction.

B = F / (qv sin θ)

Where B = magnetic field strength (Tesla), F = force, q = charge, v = velocity, θ = angle between v and B.

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines

Lines emerge from the North Pole and merge at the South Pole outside the magnet.

Inside the magnet, direction is from South Pole to North Pole — forming continuous closed loops.

Closer field lines → stronger magnetic field. Widely spaced → weaker field.

Critical Rule: No two magnetic field lines ever intersect. If they did, a compass needle at the intersection would point in two directions simultaneously — physically impossible.

SECTION 02

Conductor Geometries & Induced Fields

1. Straight Wire

A current-carrying straight conductor produces a magnetic field in the form of concentric circles centred at every point along the wire.

B = μ₀I / (2πr)

Where μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A (permeability of free space), I = current (A), r = distance from wire (m).

RIGHT-HAND THUMB RULE

Hold the wire in your right hand with the thumb pointing along the current. Your curled fingers show the direction of the magnetic field lines.

2. Circular Current Loop

Magnetic field lines form concentric loops around the wire. At the centre of the loop, field lines appear straight and parallel — indicating a uniform field direction.

B = μ₀I / (2R)

Where R = radius of the loop. For N turns: B = Nμ₀I / (2R).

3. Solenoid

A coil of many circular turns of insulated copper wire wrapped in a cylinder. The magnetic field pattern of a solenoid is nearly identical to that of a bar magnet. The interior field is uniform and parallel.

B = μ₀nI

Where n = number of turns per unit length. A ferromagnetic core (soft iron) inserted inside creates an electromagnet.

Force on Conductor & Fleming's Rules

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in an external magnetic field, it experiences a mechanical force. This force is maximum when the current is perpendicular to the field, and zero when parallel.

F = BIL sin θ

SECTION 05

Electric Motor

How It Works

An electric motor converts electrical energy → mechanical energy. It consists of:

Rectangular coil — placed between poles of a permanent magnet (the armature).

Split-ring commutator — reverses current direction every half rotation to maintain continuous unidirectional rotation.

Carbon brushes — maintain electrical contact with the commutator rings.

Permanent magnet — provides the external field B.

PRINCIPLE

When a rectangular coil carrying current I is placed in field B, forces F = BIL act on each side. The opposing forces on opposite sides create a torque that rotates the coil.

τ = BINA sin α

τ = torque, N = turns, A = area, α = angle between coil plane and field

SECTION 06

Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's Discovery

The phenomenon of generating an electric current in a circuit by changing the magnetic flux linked with it is called Electromagnetic Induction (EMI).

ε = −N × (ΔΦ/Δt)

ε = induced EMF, N = turns, ΔΦ/Δt = rate of change of magnetic flux.

Lenz's Law

The direction of the induced current is always such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that caused it. (Conservation of energy.)

AC Generator

Converts mechanical → electrical energy. The rotating coil in a field produces a sinusoidally alternating EMF:

ε = ε₀ sin(ωt)

SECTION 07

Domestic Electric Circuits

Three-Wire System (India)

Power is supplied to Indian households through a three-wire transmission at 220 V AC at 50 Hz.

Live Wire — Red Insulation

Carries high potential (220 V). It is the "hot" wire that supplies current to appliances.

Neutral Wire — Black Insulation

Maintained at 0 V. Completes the circuit loop back to the power station.

Earth Wire — Green Insulation

Connected to a metal plate buried in the earth. Provides a safe low-resistance path for leakage currents, protecting users from electric shock.

Safety Mechanisms

⚡ Short Circuit

Occurs when live and neutral wires make direct contact due to damaged insulation. Resistance drops to near zero → enormous current surge → fire hazard.

R → 0, so I = V/R → ∞

🔌 Overloading

Occurs when too many high-power appliances operate simultaneously, drawing total current exceeding the safe rating of home wiring.

🛡 Fuse / MCB

A fuse wire of low melting point breaks the circuit when current exceeds safe limits. Modern homes use MCBs (Miniature Circuit Breakers) that trip automatically and can be reset.

Safe current rating: 5A, 15A, 20A lines
